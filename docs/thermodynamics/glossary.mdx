## Glossary

<p>
  <strong>anion</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/305.dfa3300d.mp3' /> - a negatively charged ion.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>bond dissociation enthalpy</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/306.a2266b1f.mp3' /> – the energy required to break a
  particular bond. A cycle used to calculate the enthalpy of formation of an ionic
  compound. The formation is considered to be broken down into 5 steps: atomization
  of the metal, atomization of the non-metal, ionization of the metal, electron affinity
  of the non-metal, and lattice formation.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>cation</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/307.033ca700.mp3' /> - a positively charged ion.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>covalent character</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/308.b73d1d68.mp3' /> – covalent character occurs in ionic
  compounds where some of the electron density is shared between the two ions.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>ΔG</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/309.87400db6.mp3' /> – ‘Delta G’, the symbol used to represent
  Gibbs free energy change.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>ΔH</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/310.b3f60aed.mp3' /> – ‘Delta H’, the symbol used to represent
  enthalpy change.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>ΔS</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/311.abda14af.mp3' /> – ‘Delta S’, the symbol used to represent
  entropy change.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>ΔGsol</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/312.19db3f2f.mp3"' /> – the Gibbs free energy change for
  dissolving.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>endothermic reaction</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/313.cb674175.mp3' /> – a chemical reaction during which
  heat energy is taken in from the surroundings. ΔH for the reaction is positive.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>enthalpy</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/314.e420cc85.mp3' /> - a measure of the energy stored
  in (or heat content of) a system. Given the symbol H.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>enthalpy change (for a reaction)</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/315.16b9780b.mp3' /> – the heat energy exchange between
  the reacting system and its surroundings at constant pressure. Given the symbol
  ΔH.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>entropy</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/316.3a603d1b.mp3' /> – a measure of disorder, given the
  symbol S. The units of S are: J K–1 mol–1
</p>
<p>
  <strong>entropy change (for a reaction)</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/317.9ff8cd90.mp3' /> – the total entropy of the products
  of a reaction minus the total entropy of the reactants.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>exothermic reaction</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/318.2eae5140.mp3' /> – a chemical reaction during which
  heat energy is given out to the surroundings. ΔH for the reaction is negative.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>Gibbs free energy change</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/319.87d01e65.mp3' /> – a quantity taking into account
  the thermodynamic factors affecting whether or not a reaction takes place spontaneously.
  ΔG = ΔH – TΔS and a reaction is spontaneous {'if ΔG < 0'}.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>Hess cycle</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/320.3f9a72aa.mp3' /> – a diagram showing a reaction taking
  place by two or more alternative routes, all of which involve the same enthalpy
  change, according to Hess's law. Also called an enthalpy cycle.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>Hess's Law</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/321.1aa4ca9d.mp3' /> – a rule stating that the overall
  enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route the reaction takes.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>lattice dissociation enthalpy</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/322.65589cd9.mp3' /> – the enthalpy changes to break apart
  one mole of solid ionic compound into its constituent gaseous ions. This quantity
  has the same magnitude but opposite sign to the lattice formation enthalpy.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>lattice formation enthalpy</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/323.9f23a9d9.mp3' /> – the enthalpy changes for the formation
  of one mole of solid ionic compound from its constituent gaseous ions. This quantity
  has the same magnitude but opposite sign to the lattice dissociation enthalpy.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>mean bond enthalpy</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/324.a6613452.mp3' /> – the average (mean) bond dissociation
  enthalpy for a particular bond in a range of different compounds. Precisely, it
  is the average enthalpy change for breaking one mole of a particular bond in a
  range of different compounds in the gas phase.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>polarizable</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/325.9953da2e.mp3' /> – the more easily polarizable an
  anion is, the more easily its electron density can be distorted by positive charge.
  Large ions are more polarizable than smaller ions.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>polarizing</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/326.51538c30.mp3' /> – a cation is polarizing if it is
  able to draw electron density away from an anion, distorting the anion's electron
  density. Small and highly charged cations are the most polarizing.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>spontaneous reaction</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/327.c6bbf2ca.mp3' /> – a reaction that tends to occur
  of its own accord.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard conditions</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/328.ae117a12.mp3' /> – the conditions under which standard
  enthalpy changes are measured. These are 298 K and 100 kPa.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard enthalpy of atomization</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/329.1d3e192e.mp3' /> – the enthalpy changes when 1 mole
  of gaseous atoms of a substance are formed from the substance in its standard state
  under standard conditions.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard enthalpy of combustion</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/330.4835850a.mp3' /> – the enthalpy changes when 1 mole
  of a substance undergoes complete combustion under standard conditions, with all
  substances being in their standard states.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard enthalpy of electron affinity</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/331.4a065f92.mp3' /> – the standard enthalpy of first
  electron affinity is the enthalpy change for the conversion of one mole of gaseous
  atoms into one mole of gaseous anions, each atom gaining one electron, under standard
  conditions, with all substances being in their standard states. Subsequent electron
  affinities represent the addition of further electrons to the gaseous anions.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard enthalpy of formation</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/332.7f847ac1.mp3' /> – the enthalpy changes when 1 mole
  of a substance is formed from its elements under standard conditions, with all
  substances being in their standard states.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard enthalpy of hydration</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/333.9f63b891.mp3' /> – the enthalpy changes when one mole
  of gaseous ions is converted to one mole of aqueous ions under standard conditions,
  with all substances being in their standard states.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard enthalpy of solution</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/334.081189ad.mp3' /> – the enthalpy changes when one mole
  of an ionic compound is dissolved in water to produce aqueous ions under standard
  conditions, with all substances being in their standard states.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard ionization enthalpy</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/335.e43a9847.mp3' /> – the first standard ionization enthalpy
  is the enthalpy change for the conversion of 1 mole of gaseous atoms to 1 mole
  of gaseous cations, each atom losing an electron, under standard conditions with
  all substances in their standard states. Subsequent ionization enthalpies represent
  removing further electrons from the gaseous cations.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>standard enthalpy of electron affinity</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/336.86a09474.mp3' /> – the standard enthalpy of first
  electron affinity is the enthalpy change for the conversion of one mole of gaseous
  atoms into one mole of gaseous anions, each atom gaining one electron, under standard
  conditions, with all substances being in their standard states. Subsequent electron
  affinities represent the addition of further electrons to the gaseous anions.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>thermodynamics</strong>
  <AudioButton url='/media/337.4ed8b873.mp3' /> – a branch of study concerned with
  the transfer of heat and work and the conversion between heat and other forms of
  energy. In its modern form it is based on four laws, the zeroth, first, second
  and third laws of thermodynamics.
</p>
